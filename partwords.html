Words from invited speakers at Sisterhood Celebration

Gloria Steinem:

  • "Races are only won by long-distance runners and that's what Sisterhood is, and that's what we need.... Because as Gerda Lerner has always pointed out to us, a movement that doesn't last at least a century leaves no impact."
  • "Sisterhood, like other women's bookstores around this country, is really a community center. It's really a place where you go to find out that you're not crazy, the system is crazy."
  • "It's in our long term interest as readers to make sure that Sisterhood and all our independent and especially our feminist bookstores continue."
  • "The minute God looks like the ruling class, you know you're in deep shit. I think we should just declare Sisterhood Bookstore the first bookstore of the post-patriarchal, post-racist, post-nationalist, post-monotheistic age. That was an experiment that failed. We're just going to go on from there."
  • "Part of what the women's movement is about is becoming each other's mothers."

Writer, editor and activist, Gloria Steinem is well-known as a lecturer, feminist organizer and frequent spokeswoman on issues of equality. Besides having authored several best-selling books, her writings have appeared in a large number of anthologies, newspapers and magazines, including New York Magazine--a weekly she helped to found in 1968, and for which she was a political columnist until 1972. Her collection Outrageous Acts and Everyday Rebellions was re-released by Henry Holt in 1995. Currently, she is a writer and consulting editor for Ms. magazine, the international feminist bi-monthly that she co-founded in 1972.

As an organizer, Steinem has helped to found the Women's Action Alliance, a national Center for information and advocacy; the National Women's Political Caucus; and the Coalition of Labor Union Women. She is president of Voters for Choice, an independent bi-partisan political action committee that supports candidates working for reproductive freedom, and she is the founding president of the Ms. Foundation for Women, which supports grassroots projects that empower women and girls.

Steinem has also co-produced an Emmy-winning TV documentary for HBO and a original TV movie for Lifetime. She is presently working, with Rosilyn Heller, on a cable drama about an acquaintance rape case and a mini-series tracing the women of two families, one white and one black, from the suffragist/abolitionist era to the beginning of modern feminism. She has been honored with numerous honorary degrees and awards for writing and journalism, as well as for her activism. In 1995, Parenting magazine selected her for its Lifetime Achievement Award for her work in promoting girls' self-esteem.

Jackie Goldberg

  • "It's important for us all to realize that the way things are set up in the financial capitalist world is to get rid of competition. This in spite of the fact that they love, the economists of capitalism, to talk about the fact that it's competition that makes it great. But it is really doublespeak of the highest order. Because the whole point of most large capitalist enterprises is to buy up--and to push out who they can't buy--all of the available competition...."
  • "I can tell you for a fact that when there are no small and feminist bookstores like Sisterhood, when you go to those big chains, the smaller voices, the more radical voices, the more independent voices, the voices of the new writers will not be there. They don't make them money and they don't need them. But we do. We need them. And they nourish us. They make another generation of women and men powerful to resist the gobbledygook that passes for small-t truth in our society."

Jackie Goldberg was first elected to public office in 1983 as a member of the Los Angeles Board of Education, where she served until 1991. She was president of the Board from 1989-91. In 1993, Goldberg was elected to the Los Angeles City Council, where she became its first openly lesbian Councilmember. Prior to holding public office, Jackie taught high school in the Compton and Los Angeles Unified School Districts.

Maxine Waters

  • "Books are powerful. It takes us a while to understand all the power centers and what has happened historically to keep women in their place and how we have been left out and marginalized. For those women understood the power of the written word, the power of books, and were able to organize a bookstore and to bring that power together in wonderful ways with so many women expressing who they were and who they are and their thoughts and their visions of what they can be."

Congresswoman Maxine Waters--the chair of the 39-member Congressional Black Caucus and vice chair of the influential Democratic Steering Committee of the House of Representatives--is considered by many to be one of the most powerful women in American politics today. She has gained a reputation as a fearless and outspoken advocate for women, children, people of color and poor people. Elected to her fourth term in the House of Representatives, Congresswoman Waters represents California's 35th District, which includes a large part of South Central Los Angeles, as well as Gardena, Hawthorne and Inglewood. As a member of the House Banking committee, Rep. Waters has won provisions to give women and minority-owned firms preference in acquiring failed financial institutions in under-served areas.

Prior to her election to the U.S. House of Representatives in 1990, Congresswoman Maxine Waters , who served 14 years in the California State Assembly, had already attracted national attention for her "no nonsense, no-holds barred" style of politics. She was responsible for some of the boldest legislation California has ever seen, including landmark affirmative action legislation which opened up state procurement and contracting opportunities to women- and minority-owned businesses. She also created the nation's first statewide Child Abuse Prevention Training Program.. She has served on the Democratic National Committee since 1980 and has been a key leader in several presidential campaigns. She is a co-founder of the Black Women's Forum, a nonprofit organization of over 1,200 African American women in the Los Angeles area and has been a grassroots leader on several local projects. Long active in the women's movement, Rep. Waters has given encouragement and financial support to women seeking public office.

Sheila Kuehl

  • " People who write: there is something so dangerous about them, so wonderfully, wonderfully dangerous!"
  • "As you know, we have to come out to be known. And it's very much the same with feminism: we had to come out about it. And it was dangerous, and it was risk-taking and it was so wonderful. I mean, we really did believe that we would get hurt, we believed we might be physically hurt, and many, many, many of us were."
  • "The right wing is really, really afraid of many, many things. But I have to tell you one of them is the ability of young people to read."
  • "There is an enormous fear that our young people will learn their real history and how to think for themselves. I mean, a free education is really a threatening thing to fascism.
  • "Feminism, unlike many other theories of social organization, is truly a transformational set of theories.
  • "I love science fiction, which incidentally in my opinion is one of the great feminist genres in the world today."
  • "You must take power in every arena that affects peoples's lives. You must never say to me, 'Oh, I don't want to run for office. It's too dirty.' Like what you do isn't..."

Sheila James Kuehl, now in her second legislative term in the California State Assembly, is the first woman to be named Speaker pro Tempore of that body. A former pioneering civil rights attorney and law professor, Ms. Kuehl represents the 41st Assembly District in Los Angeles County. She chairs the Select Committee on California's Women and serves on the California Coastal Conservancy, the State Commission on the Status of Women, the Domestic Violence Program Advisory, and the Select Committee on Entertainment and the Arts as well as on the Appropriations, Higher Education, Human Services, Judiciary and Information Technology Committees.

During her first term in the Assembly, Ms. Kuehl authored 23 bills that were signed into law to further protect domestic violence victims and their children, tighten child support enforcement, prohibit discrimination on the basis of gender and sexual orientation in the workplace and in education, safeguard the environment, and increase the rights of crime victims. Last year George magazine selected her as one of the twenty most fascinating women in politics.

Prior to her election to the Assembly , Ms. Kuehl drafted and fought to get into California law more than forty pieces of legislation relating to children, families, women, and discrimination. She was a law professor at Loyola, UCLA and USC Law Schools and co-founded and served as managing attorney of the California Women's Law Center. She also served as President of the Women Lawyers' Association of Los Angeles and as a member of the California Supreme Court's Advisory Committee on Gender Bias in the Courts. In her youth, she was known for her role as Zelda Gilroy in the television series "The Many Loves of Dobie Gillis."

bell hooks

  • "The heartbeat of women's bookstores is feminism and feminist politics. In so many ways, it's a triumph of feminism that places like Borders are trying to compete."
  • "I remember 25 years ago when places in the mainstream would not carry our books, so that first and foremost feminist bookstores were the true embodiment of democratic freedom of speech."

bell hooks is the author of fifteen books, including Killing Rage, Ain't I a Woman, and Bone Black: Memories of Girlhood. Born in Kentucky and educated at Stanford and UC Santa Cruz, hooks now lives in New York City, where she is Distinguished Professor of English at City College in New York. hooks' brilliant writings explore feminist issues with courage and insight. As Dr. Maya Angelou notes, "Each offering from bell hooks is a major event, as she has so much to give us." Her latest book, Wounds of Passion: a Writing Life, has recently been published by Henry Holt and Company.

The opening lines of the preface of hooks' new memoir speak to the theme of today's event and the relevance of her participation:

"To be born a girl in the fifties and years before was to enter a world where folks still believed that the most important event of a young woman's coming of age was marriage. Those of us who were not fantasizing about a white wedding and the man of our dreams knew we were freaks. We knew better than to speak our longings. From age ten on I dreamed of becoming a writer. Books were my ecstasy and just as I wanted to curl up in my tiny attic room bed and be transported far away, I dreamed of writing words that would offer someone else the same pleasure."

Emily Levine

  • "In law, you take an enormous, complex problem like crime and reduce it to a baseball slogan: 'Three strikes and you're out!' This is where, also, men have the edge, of course. If women had known, if we had called it a 'personal foul' instead of 'sexual harassment...'"
  • "I think [Freud] is right about penis envy. I think there is such a thing. I think he's wrong about who has it."
  • "Tweny-five years ago I had a man say to me, 'I will consider women my equals the day they can stand up next to me in line and piss against a wall....' You wonder what kind of great thinking comes out of a statement like that. Possibly the trickle-down theory..."

In addition to her numerous appearances as a comedienne, Emily Levine has worked in television as a writer/producer, working on shows such as "Designing Women" and "Love and War." She is currently with Norman Lear's Act II Productions at Paramount where she is writing a pilot for NBC. Cited as one of the new "Queens of Comedy" by Newsweek magazine, Emily has performed on a number of television shows, including "Late Night With David Letterman."

Eloise Klein Healy

  • "Does anybody in this place not know why you're here today?"
  • "Before the Internet, there was the women's net, and Sisterhood Bookstore was there at the beginning to organize the community, keep it running and keep it informed. Thank Goddess, Sisterhood is still with us!"

Eloise Klein Healy is the author of four books of poetry, including Artemis in Echo Park (which is also available on CD). She is the founding chair of the new MFA in Creative Writing Program at Antioch University, Los Angeles. And she is an Associate Editor and Poetry Editor of The Lesbian Review of Books.

Michele Sernos

  • "When Chicana Falsa was first published, the press folded. I remember having a stack of 1000 books and nowhere to go, and I piled them into my Honda, and I was going from bookstore to bookstore. I remember right away going to the chain bookstores, not really knowing where to take these books, and was met with so much question, so much negativity. I took the books to Sisterhood Bookstore. I remember Adele taking my bio.... She was very receptive and very supportive, and I want to thank you so much for that. It meant so much to me."

Chicana writer Michele Serros had her first book, Chicana Falsa and Other Stories of Death, Identity and Oxnard, published when she was a UCLA undergraduate. Her poetry and short stories have also been included in several anthologies, and her second book, How to be a Chicana Role Model, is slated for publication by Riverhead books. An award-winning commentator on public radio, Michele's performances have been described as "part poetry reading, part stand-up comedy, part theatrical event."

Robin Tyler

  • "The radical right still thinks that homosexuality is a disease. If homosexuality is a disease, we should all call in sick to work: "Hello, can't work today, still queer."
  • "Jerry Falwell...thinks global warming was caused by the AIDS quilt."
  • "Thank you, Sisterhood.... Thank you for being there before being a lesbian and being a feminist was so commercial. Thank you for all of your hard work. Just thank you. Thank you for being our sisters."
