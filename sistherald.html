"We All Love Books And We Are All UCLA Graduates"

A Book Store Especially for Ms.

By Camilla Snyder

Herald-Examiner Staff Writer

It seems inevitable that a movement which spawned its own magazine, 'Ms.', and its own writers: Millet, de Beauvoir, Morgan, Reeves, Greer, friedan, et al, would eventually also bring forth its own bookshop. It has happened right here in West Los Angeles.

For those who shun the strident, man-hating breed of feminists who seem to abound in the women's liberation movement, the trio of young women who opened the Sisterhood Bookstore come as a welcome relief. All three Ms's, Simone Gold, Adele Wallace and Gayen Phillips are also Mrs.'s; what's more, they are happily married. Mrs. Gold and Mrs. Phillips have children and Mrs. Wallace expects her first child any day now.

The Sisterhood partners are an 'in-bred' group; Mrs. Gold and Mrs. Wallace are sisters-in-law while Gayen Phillips is Simone Gold's cousin-in-law. The group have intellectual as well as family ties which is why they chose to open a feminist book shop in place of a feminist dress shop or game shop.

"We all love books, and we are all UCLA graduates," Mrs. Gold whose B.A. is in English explained. "Adele has a degree in elementary education while Gayen has a degree in psychology."

In addition to her training in matters psychological, Phillips has a talent for carpentry which is both impressive and apropos in these non-sexist times. She designed, and with her partners' help made, all the book shelves for the back-off-the-street shop at 1915 3/4 Westwood Blvd.

"We wanted to put our capital (each partner contributed $1000 to the shop) into stock," Gayen explained. "So I went to a lumber yard with my blueprints, selected pine which we stained, and built the shelves. You will notice that each shelf slants just enough so that the titles are easy to read and books easy to get at."

The Sisterhood Bookstore opened quietly compared to the razzamatazz of the recent Brentano debut here; coffee and cookies, chips and dips which the girls made themselves were offered to guests, and the opening had a style -- sort of homespun intellectual -- of its own.

Now that the partners have been in business a full month, they have sorted out their pleasures and their problems. "We are pleased that we have so many men customer," 30-year old Adele Wallace remarked. "I would estimate that 70 per cent of our clientele are women while 30 per cent are men. And we have an unusual number of children on our books."

Non-sexist children's books, that is volumes which show boys and girls in a variety of roles are a big feature of the shop. As Simone Gold explains:

"In our children's books, the girls do not always stand watch as the boys toboggan down the hill. In so many children's books the girls watch, to boys do. Girls seldom show qualities of courage, ingenuity. By the same token boys seldom show qualities of caring for people or artistic talents like painting. Everything in most children's books is so rigid. Girls are emotional, boys are clever when we know that all people are both."

Adele Wallace noted that a book called, 'Quiet on Account of Dinosaur,' was a good example of what a non-sexist children's book should be. "It's about a little girl who discovers a dinosaur. She sees his tail outside a cage, pulls on the tail. Now you have to admit that's a courageous thing for a little girl -- or for anybody -- to do. The little girl in this book becomes an expert on dinosaurs. She goes on and becomes a museum expert on dinosaurs, ends up with a Ph.D. and a curatorial job at a museum."

It seems unlikely that Mmes. Gold, Wallace or Phillips will go on for Ph.D.'s of their own; they are far too fascinated with their business prospects.

"We just can't stay away from the store," Simone Gold said adding that the 10 a.m through 6 p.m. hours, Monday through Saturday, did make some involved babysitting and housekeeping arrangements necessary.

There are plenty of shop 'chores' to keep the three occupied. Authors come in for advice on who might publish their books, artists stop in about exhibits of their works. Then there are book buying chores.

"Getting books is the big problem," Simone Gold explains. "Publishers in New York are so far away. A lot of times they aren't very cooperative. They refuse to deal in cash. We will send a check and say 'If you want to wait until it clears before sending our books fine.' Now we have a credit rating with the Central Publishing People so things are better. Then, too, the Feminist Press and a group called Lollipop Power in Virginia plus Schockin Publishing in New York are fine to deal with."

A poster on the wall of the Sisterhood Bookstore features a diaper ad reprint which reads 'The Future President of the United States Deserves a Drier Bottom.' Another poster proselytizes "There will never be a truly liberated man until there are liberated women." The Sisterhood Bookstore would seem to be dispensing brotherhood and idealistic parenthood along with a nourshing diet of reading material (72 titles, mostly paperback, plus magazines and some non-sexist games) for those in the West Los Angeles area. And the shop owners already speak of opening their second shop. After all, the first month's gross was $3000. Not bad for a business capitalized at $3000 in an out of the way location.
